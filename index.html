State of Forests in Sri Lanka

Sri Lanka

Introduction

Sri Lanka is a tropical island off the coast of India, its location near the equator means that it is home to large quantities of tropical forests. However, due to modernisation efforts and a growing population, forests cover has been decreasing. Unfortunately due to these reasons it is one of 25 biodiversity hotspots, it is important that Sri Lanka manages to cease these activities as Sri Lankan forests are extremely diverse, infact they have the highest biodiversity density in Asia.

Deforestation

As mentioned previously deforestation is an ongoing problem in Sri Lanka, much like other developing countries in the tropical region. Unfortunately, Sri Lanka has had vast amounts of deforestation occurring over the last century or so. In the 1920's, the country had a large forest cover of about 49%, when measured in 2005, it was only at 26%, nearly half of the forest cover available in 1920. Not only does deforestation threaten biodiversity, it also has lead to soil erosion and is a common cause of dangerous landslides, especially in the mountainous Nuwara Eliya region. 

Conservation efforts

The president has stated that the forest cover has increased over the last 4 years as a result of government policies to increase forest cover. There have been multiple programs aimed at increasing forest cover in order to help combat climate change and increase biodiversity. The government has been active in the conservation movement since the 1970s and recognizes the importance of Sri Lanka's incredibly biodiverse forests and has established reserves to protect biodiversity hotspots, one such example is the Sinharaja forest reserve established to protect the then endangered lowland rainforests. The government infact has dedicated an entire department labelled the Department of Forest Conservation, this department has been directly responsible for the management of Sinharaja forest, Hurulu forest and Kanneliya-Dediyagala-Nakiyadeniya forest. This department has powers in place to stop illegal poaching and logging that may take place.

On top of these policies, the government has also added policies to enable sustainable logging practices, companies are now able to lease publicly owned land given that their logging practices are sustainable. These policies not only protects the tree but also is aimed at prevention a loss of biodiversity, soil eroition and conserve water.

Plantations

Although there might have been some effective conservation efforts in place as explored previously, one important fact that hasnt been addressed yet is the fact that Sri Lankan forests are not all natural. Sri Lanka is an exporter of rubber, cinnamons and other spices. This means that there is heavy incentive to replace natural rainforests with plantations in order for private companies to gain more revenue, infact only 60% of Sri Lanka's forests were natural forest, with the rest being made of plantations. This is a stark contrast to Australia, where 98% are native forests. 

Indigenous people

Sri Lanka is home to an indigenous people known as Wanniya-Laeto (Veddas), the name literally translates to people of the forest. Due to the state's management of forests, indigenous people have been pushed out of their natural habitat and forced against their will to live a modern lifestyle. Their way of life was made illegal in order for the state to manage public parks, this came at a cost not only to these peoples way of life but also a cost to their health as they were not able to rely upon hunting for their food. 

Outlook

Sri Lanka's state of forests are generally heading in a positive direction through government policies put in place to help conserve forest and ensure biodiversity, however, it is critically important to ensure that the government takes care of its Indigenous people and find a compromise to allow them to live as they have for thousands of years. It is imperative that foreign agencies put pressure on Sri Lanka to give Indigenous people their rights back.
